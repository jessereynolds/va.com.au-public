NOISING IN BLACK+WHITE 
The photograph has stood the test of time since the advent of the motion picture, long after many expected it to go the way of the horse and cart. A cinematographer can capture hours of real-time action with peripheral extras like sound and now audience interaction, yet a stills cameraperson only has a split second to communicate her/his message. 
One guesses that the reason for its longevity is because we still crave imaginative free-reign over our art, creating an entire narrative from one single idea, or just spontaneously digging what we see.

And giving the young people of Australia free reign over their imagination was exactly what was asked for when a noisy call went out for submissions to Black+White, the Oz magazine famous for its world-class photography and an uncanny ability to convince famous people to bare their bits in the name of art...

From books of quotes and self-portraits to rundown railways and ballet shoes, the subject matter varied like the seasons thanks to the project's only guideline - to capture your life and world.

"What struck us was how accurately the images reflected contemporary tastes and trends," comments Karen-Jane Eyre, Black+White Editor.

"The works displayed a remarkably mature intent and were of a very high technical standard. Additionally, there were a significant number of strong entries from female photographers, which is a positive sign of things to come. For our final selection we chose some images for their pop sensibility, some for their originality and others for their sheer artistry."

Most applicants are aspiring amateurs and students, like Melbourne creative writing undergraduate Emily Hart. "It's always been a dream of mine to express myself," says Emily. "I must say, I put a lot more effort into photography than my writing."

Emily's "The Dancer" can be seen alongside 23-year-old photography student Cory White's "In The Dark", a gorgeously evocative and considered work from a person who has just finished a degree in criminology and loitered in darkrooms since the age of 12.

Tracey Grimson, noise Print Producer, comments further on the depth of talent, not just in the Black+White call out, but across the festival. "We've found literally thousands of young people through noise who are producing high quality creative works and who have interesting stories to tell, yet who aren't yet culturally or professionally recognised."

Sydney film student Chris Coucouvinis also has his "Fold" and "Soar" works on display in the supplement, a guy who compounds his visual creativity with heartily honest words.

"I think a lot, sometimes too much and over analyse everything," Chris mentions in his noise artist profile. "I'm not the guy behind the guy behind the guy, I'm probably the guy who just dropped his change all over the floor across the room... I like to observe people. I think we are the strangest creatures on this planet, but I like being part of that."

Chris can rest assured people will be observing him and enjoying this strange planet this October, as Black+White's noise supplement, full of zesty surprises, appears on newsstands.
