A quick history lesson:

Haiku is a form of contemplative poetry that celebrates nature, colour, season, contrasts and surprises. Usually it has three lines and seventeen syllables disseminated in five, seven and five. It uses simple expressions in ways that allow deeply felt emotions and a sense of discovery to be readily conveyed to the reader. Haiku's structure is intended to transcend the limitation imposed by the usual language and the linear/scientific thinking that treat the nature and the human being as a machine. It is ironic then that the two disjointed pieces of haiku that feature in this work were composed entirely by a computer, who writes as if it were a human. The mysterious, emotive words stencilled across the work mean to blur the distinction between human and machine, and suggest that perhaps human creativity will become pedestrian and obsolete, a silent observer to new forms of digital imagination and expressionism.
