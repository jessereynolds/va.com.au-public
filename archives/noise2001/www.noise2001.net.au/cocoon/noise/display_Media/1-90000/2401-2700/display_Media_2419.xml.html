SECONDS FOR NOBODY

When invited to a party in the 'and friend' capacity, I tend to assume the worst. Walking toward a dinner party set up in this manner makes me feel no different, but my hesitation is proved to be a calm before a ridiculous storm.

My partner and I enter a share house furnished in atypical student trash-chic, and sit at a long table, with a couple of her friends from college, and many faces new to both me and her. We are late, some small relief I think as I sit and am greeted moments later with a plate of risotto mush. Introductions are made but it's not long before those assembled make a scene similar to what those in the third world must appear like, after not eating for a long, long period. And between servings, the third world is the first off the rank of meaningful conversational topics, those that can only be voiced with the authority of people with backgrounds where an undergraduate lifestyle is considered the only glitch in an otherwise well-above-poverty line existence.

So after a half-hour of 'god isn't it terrible how we forget Kosovo', I retreat to the balcony and draw deeply on a cigarette. I have been silent for the majority of the evening and am aware that my partner's friends will chastise her if I don't make an effort (rule of the new couple #16). So I think of a conversational tangent, and head inside to implement it, with the requisite glee of a undertaker...

In the hall outside the main dining area I find the bottle that we bought, which has been filed in a cupboard underneath the telephone, for later consumption. I swig heartily, and enter the room with a little extra courage, ready for action. Sadly this helps little, as the scene inside requires more courage than that cupboard contains.

All of the dinner guests have chosen to refuse their dinners, and are in the midst of reaching as far as possible down their throats. The noise is like nothing I've heard before, the floor spotted with buckets, holding the collective spewtum of 15 sincere activists; - if only for this evening, they're reaching out to the poor and under-nourished, as massive attack play on the large stereo in the background. And just as the naturally queasy cannot stand the sight of vomit and reciprocate, I join the fray and spill my meal, impossibly more mushy second time around, over a collection of marketing strategy textbooks. One from the group rushes to me and, before wiping her face, pats my back heartily and says something like 'doesn't affirmative action feel so cleansing?!?'
