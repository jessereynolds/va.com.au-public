blurb  // WHERE IT ALL STARTED

I started drawing comics in 1996. I was involved in the creation of a magazine at uni, and someone suggested putting in comics - for some reason the idea appealed to me and I made up a character called Lima the witch. Lima had a pet cat who she flew around with at night. I gave up on Lima pretty quickly though, when I realised that I preferred writing about things that had actually happened to me.

I had a crazy experience involving acid that I decided to draw a comic about, and from then on I started drawing comics about things that had happened to me. I got a few early comics published in Voiceworks magazine. Last year I went up to the Young Writers festival in Newcastle, and I decided to put together a small comic to take up there. That's how Nervous Energy was born - it's a comic about being nervous that comes with a floppy disk. On the disk is The Nerve Game - a computer game I made about stress that is in the same style as my comics.

THE CREATIVE PROCESS

I love writing comics - I go through phases where I don't do any then I start and draw heaps. Because I'm pretty busy doing animation and installation art I often don't get time to draw comics. Then I find that for some reason (usually a deadline, or a project I'm involved in) I start again, and at first it's really hard. But as soon as I start drawing again I start getting lots of ideas and it gets a lot easier. I think my off-and-on output is also related to the fact that I draw very personal comics, and there are times in my life where I feel like telling stories about my life, and other times when I don't. To be able to write about personal stuff you need a certain amount of detachment, particularly with really full-on stuff. So sometimes I find I'm still too close to a topic to write about it.

The hardest thing about drawing comics is the painful time when you ink the comic and you have to concentrate so hard so you don't make a mistake! Also trying to work out how to say something that's really hard to say.

The best thing is being able to see your experiences in a whole new light; drawing comics with friends. A friend and I have regular comic nights where we'll draw comics together. It's much more fun to draw with someone else, and it motivates you. Also you can get feedback from each other.

SELF-PUBLISHING

The other reason I love doing comics is because I enjoy making something concrete that people can keep, especially since I do a lot of work with digital mediums and installations, which are very temporary things.

Putting together Nervous Energy is a very time consuming process. First I draw a rough copy in pencil, then I draw a good copy in pencil, then I ink it using a calligraphy pen and ink. Then I scan it into the computer, and colour it in on the computer.

Then I arrange all the pages using a different computer program, after which I print a copy out then photocopy it. The cover I did in colour, and I printed out each cover on my printer . Then because I make the comic a non-standard size, I have to cut everything out using a stanley knife then put it together and staple the pages together, I always forget how much work it takes!

AUTOBIOGRAPHY

In telling stories about stuff that has happened to me I hope people who read it can relate to parts of it. Sometimes the comics I write have a subtle message and people can choose to take it or leave it.

Personal stories can change the world - if you can relate to someone's experience it gives you a new view on things. Sometimes it's good to know you're not the only person in the world who feels a particular way, and if you read a comic where someone is going through an experience similar to one you've had it can help you understand your experience better. About a year ago my cat died, and I remember reading a comic by Roberta Gregory about her cat dying. Reading it made me really sad, but it kind of made me feel better at the same time. I love the way comics can be so funny yet so sad at the same time.

YOU DON'T HAVE TO BE ABLE TO DRAW!

I used to be really down on myself because I felt like I couldn't draw in a realistic photo-like way. And occasionally I still get frustrated - when I was drawing my comic about paperboys I kept having to draw bicycles which I found really difficult because they're hard to draw. But now I like my child-like figures - I think the simplicity of my drawings is part of their appeal.
